# Blog topic: why simplicity is invisible in promotion — and how a manager fixes it

**Engineering-culture / leadership theme** (genuinely non-AI — the diversity win from the
fresh sweep). Two linked vault notes make the case: engineering orgs reward *complexity* by
accident, because interviews, design reviews, and promotion packets make elaborate systems
visible and simple solutions invisible. The distinction that saves it from being a rant:
**earned vs. unearned complexity** ([[unearned-complexity]]) — "we're hitting DB limits and
need to shard" vs. "we might in three years, so shard now."

**The original angle (what makes it a post, not a rehash):** the source note summarizes an
external article (terriblesoftware.org). The blog post must be **Khaled's own manager-side
take** — how you actually make avoided complexity *visible* in the systems you control:
- Writing promotion packets that credit "the incident that never happened" and the 50-line
  fix, not just the reusable framework.
- Running design reviews that ask "what would the simplest version cost us?" before
  "shouldn't we future-proof this?"
- Calibration language for "operating at senior/staff level *through restraint*."
- The honest tension: you can't fully fix the incentive alone, so what levers do you have.
